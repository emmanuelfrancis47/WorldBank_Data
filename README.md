# WorldBank_Data

Ghana Poverty and Inequality Analysis (1987-2022)
Overview
This analysis examines Ghana's progress in reducing poverty and the accompanying trends in economic inequality over a 35-year period (1987-2022), using comprehensive data from the World Bank Development Indicators. The study evaluates multiple dimensions of poverty—including income-based measures, multidimensional poverty, and urban living conditions—alongside inequality metrics such as the Gini index and income share distribution across population segments.

Key Objectives
1. Track poverty reduction progress using national and international poverty lines

2. Analyze inequality trends through Gini index and income quintile shares

3. Assess urban development via slum population trends

4. Evaluate growth inclusiveness by comparing welfare changes for the bottom 40% versus the total population

4. Provide evidence-based recommendations for policy formulation


# Methodology
The analysis employs:

1. Time-series trend analysis of 15+ poverty and inequality indicators

2. Comparative bar charts showing income distribution changes between 1987 and 2016

3. Correlation analysis between poverty and inequality measures

4. Growth incidence analysis comparing income growth rates across population segments

# Data Sources
1. World Bank Development Indicators (World Bank Group)

2. Period covered: 1987-2022

3. Key indicators analyzed: Gini index, poverty headcount ratios (multiple thresholds), income share by quintile, slum population, multidimensional poverty index

# Main Findings
The analysis reveals a dual narrative: Ghana has achieved substantial poverty reduction (national poverty fell from 51.7% to 23.4% between 1991-2016) and improved urban living conditions (slum population reduced from 60.4% to 33.5%). However, this progress has been accompanied by rising inequality (Gini index increased from 35.3 to 43.5) and pro-rich growth patterns (bottom 40% experienced negative income growth of -0.19% annually during 2012-2016, while the total population grew at +1.27%).

# Policy Implications
The findings highlight an urgent need for inclusive growth strategies, strengthened social protection systems, progressive taxation, and targeted investments in human capital for underserved populations. Without addressing rising inequality, further poverty reduction will become increasingly difficult to achieve.
